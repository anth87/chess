Lesson 11 · Ruy Lopez as White

Ruy Lopez vs Old Steinitz: d5 When Black Plays …Nh7

~10 min · One fix: close the centre the moment Black's knight heads for g5

Why this lesson exists

Your 12-month records show 11+ White losses to the Ruy Lopez Old Steinitz (3...d6, ECO C62). Two recent losses — vs kretenkonj (1749, 2026-06-19) and vs annapurna317 (1870, 2026-02-27) — followed the identical pattern: Black played …Nh7, you played a passive queen move, Black went to g5, the d4 pawn fell, and your attack never materialised. The same game, played twice.

Your opening setup — h3, O-O, c3, Re1, d4 — is fine. The leak is a single decision on move 13.

Data: leak map · Case study: vs kretenkonj 2026-06-19 and vs annapurna317 2026-02-27 · ECO C62

The trigger and the mantra

After 1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5 d6 4.h3 Bd7 5.O-O Nf6 6.Re1 Be7 7.c3 O-O 8.d4 exd4 9.cxd4 a6 10.Ba4 b5 11.Bc2 h6 12.a3, Black plays 12...Nh7. Name this move when you see it. The knight is heading to g5 to attack d4.

d5 on …Nh7. The moment Black plays …Nh7, close the centre with 13.d5. Ng5 now has no target. Follow with 14.Nd2 to regroup, then f4 for the kingside push. Do not play Qd3, Ne3, or any other move — the knight is already heading to g5 and Qd3 does not stop it.

The correct response — close with d5

12...Nh7 13.d5! — then 14.Nd2 · f4

Arising from: 1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5 d6 4.h3 Bd7 5.O-O Nf6 6.Re1 Be7 7.c3 O-O 8.d4 exd4 9.cxd4 a6 10.Ba4 b5 11.Bc2 h6 12.a3 Nh7 13.d5!

After 13.d5, Black's Nc6 must retreat — it is now out of play (Na5 or Nb8, both passive). The Nh7 has nowhere useful to go. White plays 14.Nbd2 to prepare the f-pawn, then 15.f4 — a standard King's Indian–style kingside march where White has the space and Black's pieces are misplaced. Black has no counterplay because the centre is closed.

The mistake — allowing …Ng5

In both losses, White played 13.Qd3 — a developing move that looks like it defends d4 (the queen eyes d4 from d3). But it does not stop …Ng5. Black immediately plays 13...Ng5, White trades knights with 14.Nxg5 Bxg5, and now the Bg5 puts long-term pressure on d4 that the queen cannot neutralise. White panics with 15.e5, but after 15...g6 16.exd6 cxd6, the d4 pawn is simply hanging.

13.Qd3? 13...Ng5 14.Nxg5 Bxg5 15.e5 g6 16.exd6 cxd6

The e5 push achieved nothing: Black recaptured with cxd6 (not dxe5, which would lose a pawn) and d4 now hangs with no repair. In the game vs kretenkonj, the game continued 17.b4 Bf5 18.Qb3 Bxc2 19.Qxc2 Nxd4 — the knight eventually won d4, Black dominated with active pieces, and White resigned on move 33. Same outcome vs annapurna317, checkmated on move 29.

Why Qd3 fails and d5 works

The key difference: d5 removes the tactical point of …Ng5. Once the centre is closed with d5, Ng5 has nothing to attack — d4 no longer exists as a target. The knight on h7 is just a misplaced piece.

Qd3 fails because the queen on d3 looks like it defends d4, but the knight goes to g5 first before attacking d4. After the trade (Nxg5 Bxg5), d4 is under long-term structural pressure from the bishop on g5 plus the Nc6. The queen alone cannot hold it against two well-placed pieces.

  • Trigger: Black plays …Nh7
  • Response: d5 immediately — no other move
  • Follow-up: Nd2, f4, kingside storm
  • Never: Qd3 or Ne3 (passive; allow …Ng5)

Retrieval practice

Answer from memory. All options are the same length.

1. After 12...Nh7 in the Old Steinitz, what should White play on move 13?

2. After 13.d5, what is White's plan in the next two moves?

3. After 13.Qd3 Ng5 14.Nxg5 Bxg5 15.e5 g6 16.exd6 cxd6, why is White's position lost?
