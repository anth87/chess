Lesson 10 · Pirc Defence as Black

Pirc vs 150 Attack: Castle First, Then …c5

~10 min · One tangible win: stop reacting to the bishop and hit the centre instead

Why this lesson exists

Your 12-month loss map shows 14+ Pirc losses to "Main Line" systems — positions where White plays 4.Be3 · 5.Qd2 (the 150 Attack), sets up long castle, then rolls f3 · g4 · h4. Lessons 2 and 8 covered the Classical (5.Bc4) and Geller (4.f4) branches. This lesson covers the third major White system: the 150 Attack.

The recurring mistake in your games: you play 5…Ng4 trying to trade White's bishop. White retreats to g5 or f4, you push …h6 and then …g5 to drive the bishop back, and now your kingside is weakened for the rest of the game. White never had to work for those targets — you handed them over.

The 2-step mantra (after 4.Be3 · 5.Qd2)

O-O · c5. Castle first — your king needs to be safe before you start counterplay. Then immediately play …c5, hitting d4 before White can play f3 and h4. You are forcing White to react for once. Never play …Ng4 here: it achieves nothing and costs you the kingside pawn structure.

The recipe — castle, then hit back

After 5.Qd2, resist the impulse to chase the bishop. Castle. Once White commits to long castle with O-O-O, strike at once with 6…c5. The pawn attacks d4 — White must either close the centre with d5 (giving you time to organise a queenside counter) or swap on c5 and give you an open c-file. Either way White's kingside storm is delayed or defused.

5…O-O 6.O-O-O c5!

Arising from: 1.e4 d6 2.d4 Nf6 3.Nc3 g6 4.Be3 Bg7 5.Qd2 O-O 6.O-O-O

If White plays 7.d5 (closes the centre), follow with 7…e6 to crack it open again — never let White seal the position while you're still developing. If White plays 7.dxc5, recapture with 7…Qa5 (attacking c3 and pressuring the c5 pawn), keeping active play on the queenside.

The mistake — chasing the bishop costs you the kingside

Many players instinctively try 5…Ng4 after 5.Qd2 — the idea being to trade the knight for the Be3 and remove White's dark-squared bishop. White simply retreats: 6.Bg5 (or 6.Bf4). Now the knight is awkwardly placed on g4 and has no useful target. Black plays 6…h6 to challenge the bishop, White retreats to h4, and then Black pushes 7…g5 to drive the bishop away. That g5 pawn is now a permanent weakness White will attack with f3 · h4 for the rest of the game.

5…Ng4? 6.Bg5 h6 7.Bh4 g5 8.Bg3

Pattern from your losses vs halberga (2025-06-01) and others · ECO B07

Black achieved nothing with …Ng4. The Be3 was never traded. The g5 and h6 pawns are now targets for the rest of the game. White plays 9.h4 — a move that would have been impossible if Black had castled and played …c5 instead.

The universal Pirc rule on bishop trades

The Bg7 is your most important piece. It controls d4, e5, and the long diagonal. Never voluntarily exchange it or weaken its pawn cover (...g5) unless you gain something concrete in return. In the 150 Attack, the Be3 is not a threat — it is White's way of baiting you. Ignore it. Castle. Hit d4.

  • vs 4.Be3 · 5.Qd2: O-O · c5
  • vs 4.Nf3 · 5.Bc4: O-O · Ng4 · c5 (Lesson 2 — Bc4 is the target, not Be3)
  • vs 4.f4 (Geller): O-O · c5 (Lesson 8 — same lever, same rule)

Notice the theme: in every Pirc system, the answer includes O-O and …c5. Castle and hit d4. The bishop on g7 does its best work when it stays on g7.

Retrieval practice

Answer from memory. All options are the same length.

1. White plays 1.e4 d6 2.d4 Nf6 3.Nc3 g6 4.Be3 Bg7 5.Qd2. What is Black's correct 5th move?

2. After 5…O-O 6.O-O-O, what should Black play immediately?

3. After 5…Ng4 6.Bg5 h6 7.Bh4 g5 8.Bg3, what did Black gain from the …Ng4 plan?

Homework: Play 3 blitz games as Black with 1.e4 d6. If White plays 4.Be3, name the two steps before you touch a piece. After each game, note: did you play …c5 before White got h4 in?
