Pirc Geller: Strike with …c5

~10 min · One tangible win: hit d4 before White's e5 bind takes over

Why this lesson exists

Your 12-month leak map shows Pirc Modern / Geller as your top Black loss cluster alongside Classical Pirc — 35+ tagged losses, and in the pure 4.f4 branch you are nearly even (43W–45L) but drift passive too often. In your win vs Knvl you played 5…c5 on move 5 and won the exchange. In your loss vs rajandahal you allowed 5.e5, took passively, and never played …c5 — White kept the space bind with …c6 instead.

Data: leak map · Line: Chess.com — Pirc Geller 4.f4

Your 5-step mantra (3+0)

Two branches: vs 5.Nf3 play 5…c5 immediately. vs 5.e5 play 5…dxe5 and …Nd5 (that part is fine) — but still look for …c5 to hit d4 before passive …c6. Never let …c6 block your counterplay.

4.f4 Bg7 5.Nf3 c5

You followed with 6.e5 dxe5 7.fxe5 Nd5 8.dxc5, traded into a winning endgame, and collected the exchange — exactly the counterplay Geller players fear.

What went wrong — your loss vs rajandahal

This was a different Geller branch: White played 5.e5, and you answered correctly with 5…dxe5 6…Nd5. You had no …c5 opportunity on move 5 — that advice applies when White plays 5.Nf3 (your Knvl game). The drift came later: on move 10 you played …c6 while the d4 pawn was still there. 10…c5 would have hit it. You followed with 12…e6, locking your bishop in and letting White pile up on the kingside.
